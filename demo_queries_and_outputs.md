## Query 1
**Q: What are the seven principles of financial management at CLAP?**

**A:**
CLAP follows seven principles of financial management as goals to work towards:

1. **Custodianship** — Stewardship and safekeeping of the organization's resources. Custodians hold assets and funds in trust, ensuring they are used per the constitution and contractual agreements.
2. **Accountability** — The moral or legal duty to explain how funds, equipment, or authority given by a third party has been used.
3. **Transparency** — Systems must be established so all financial information is recorded accurately, presented clearly, and easily disclosed to those who have the right to request it.
4. **Consistency** — Financial systems should be consistent over the years so comparisons can be made, trends analyzed, and transparency facilitated.
5. **Integrity** — The honesty and reliability of CLAP must be beyond question. Records must be a true reflection of reality and proper procedures must be followed by all staff.
6. **Non-Deficit Financing** — CLAP should not set out to achieve its objectives until it is confident it will have sufficient funding to cover all activities.
7. **Standard Documentation** — Financial records must observe internationally accepted accounting standards. The accounting software Tally 9.0 should be understandable by any accountant from anywhere in the world.

## Query 2
**Q: What are the four building blocks of CLAP's financial management?**

**A:**
CLAP's financial management involves the following four building blocks:

1. **Keeping Records** — Basic records including contracts, letters, receipts, and debits that prove every transaction has taken place. Details of each transaction are recorded in a cashbook.
2. **Internal Control** — Controls to prevent misuse of money, including keeping cash in a bank account, authorizing all expenditure, following the planned budget, conducting financial monitoring, employing qualified finance staff, and ensuring audits and bank reconciliation.
3. **Budgeting** — Preparing accurate budgets by carefully forecasting expected spending on activities.
4. **Financial Reporting** — Writing and reviewing financial reports that summarize income and expenditure over a period of time using a system of codes and budget heads.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 3]

## Query 3
**Q: How often must bank reconciliation be done and why is it important?**

**A:**
A bank reconciliation statement must be prepared every month. It involves checking that the amount of cash in the bank matches the amount recorded in the cashbook.

This is particularly important because it proves that the amounts recorded in the cashbook and the reports based on it are accurate.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 4]

## Query 4
**Q: What internal controls does CLAP have in place?**

**A:**
CLAP has the following internal controls in place to ensure money cannot be misused:

- Keeping cash in a safe place, ideally in a bank account
- All expenditure is properly authorized
- Following the planned budget
- Financial monitoring — tracking how much money has been spent on what, every month
- Employing qualified finance staff
- Ensuring internal audit (quarterly), statutory audit (annually), FC return to the Ministry of Home Affairs, Government of India, and income tax return every year
- Preparation of a bank reconciliation statement every month

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 4]

## Query 5
**Q: What is the minimum number of people required for separation of financial duties?**

**A:**
Finance duties must be shared between at least three people. This separation of duties is required to prevent temptation to steal, reduce the opportunity to commit fraud, and to share the workload.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 5]

## Query 6
**Q: What documents must be maintained for every financial transaction?**

**A:**
A valid supporting document is required for every transaction. These documents must be securely filed and stored for the minimum period required. They serve as protection for staff, provide evidence of the transaction, and record the details of each transaction.

Additionally, the details of each transaction must also be written in a cashbook, listing how much was spent, on what, and when.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 3, 5]

## Query 7
**Q: What is CLAP's policy on reporting to donors?**

**A:**
CLAP's policy on donor reporting is as follows:

- Sending reports late is guaranteed to reduce a donor's confidence in the organization.
- CLAP always identifies the donors' reporting requirements.
- A reporting timetable is set up as per the donor's format and timing of reports.

CLAP builds donor confidence by:
- Describing plans to use funds through clear plans and budgets
- Demonstrating proper controls and good management
- Providing honest and accurate reports of work done

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 4]

## Query 8
**Q: What is the purpose of a monthly cash flow forecast?**

**A:**
A monthly cash flow forecast helps to identify and take action to avoid short-term cash flow problems before they occur. It is listed as Good Practice Standard 12 in CLAP's minimum standards for financial management.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 6]

## Query 9
**Q: What is a funding grid and when is it required?**

**A:**
A funding grid is required when more than one donor is funding an organization or project. Its purpose is to avoid double-funding situations (where the same cost is charged to two donors) and to identify areas of funding shortfall. It is listed as Good Practice Standard 14.

[Financial Policy.pdf, Page 6]

## Query 10
**Q: What is the policy on maintaining reserves?**

**A:**
CLAP's policy (Good Practice Standard 18) requires maintaining a reasonable level of reserves. This makes the organization less vulnerable to financial shocks and helps overcome short-term cash flow problems.
